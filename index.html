of Jose Rizal

About Jose Rizal

Jose Rizal, a Filipino nationalist and polymath, was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines, during the Spanish colonial period. He played a significant role in the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule through his works and activism. Here's some key information about him:

Nationalism
José Rizal's nationalism was deeply ingrained in his identity and permeated every aspect of his life. As a nationalist, Rizal held a strong sense of loyalty and devotion to the Philippines, striving for its independence and progress.

Rizal's nationalism was evident in his writings, where he passionately advocated for the rights and dignity of Filipinos under Spanish colonial rule. Through his novels, essays, and poetry, he exposed the injustices and abuses committed by the colonial regime, awakening national consciousness and fostering a sense of unity among his fellow Filipinos.

Furthermore, Rizal's nationalism was reflected in his actions and sacrifices for the cause of Philippine independence. Despite the personal risks and sacrifices involved, he dedicated his life to the struggle for freedom, demonstrating unwavering courage and determination in the face of adversity.
Patriotism
José Rizal's patriotism was deeply rooted in his profound love for the Philippines and his unwavering commitment to its liberation from colonial rule. He approached patriotism through various interconnected dimensions, demonstrating intellectual, cultural, and moral facets.

Intellectually, Rizal believed in the transformative power of education and enlightenment as catalysts for national awakening and liberation. Through his writings, particularly his seminal novels "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," he exposed the injustices of Spanish colonial rule, sparking a sense of national consciousness among his compatriots.

Culturally, Rizal celebrated the richness of Filipino heritage, recognizing it as a vital aspect of national identity. He championed Filipino customs, traditions, and languages, viewing them as sources of strength and resilience in the face of oppression. His cultural pride fostered a sense of unity and solidarity among Filipinos, contributing to the collective spirit of resistance.

In terms of resistance, Rizal advocated for non-violent means of struggle, emphasizing the importance of peaceful reform and dialogue. Despite witnessing the injustices perpetrated by the colonial regime, he remained steadfast in his commitment to effecting change through moral persuasion rather than violent confrontation. His advocacy for non-violence sought to unite rather than divide the Filipino people in their quest for freedom.

Backgrounds


Parents & Siblings

The Rizals is considered one of the biggest families during their time. Domingo Lam-co, the family's paternal ascendant was a full-blooded Chinese who came to the Philippines from Amoy, China in the closing years of the 17th century and married a Chinese half-breed by the name of Ines de la Rosa. Researchers revealed that the Mercado-Rizal family had also traces of Japanese, Spanish, Malay and Even Negrito blood aside from Chinese. Jose Rizal came from a 13-member family consisting of his parents, Francisco Mercado II and Teodora Alonso Realonda, and nine sisters and one brother.

FRANCISCO MERCADO (1818-1898)

Father of Jose Rizal who was the youngest of 13 offsprings of Juan and Cirila Mercado. Born in Biñan, Laguna on April 18, 1818; studied in San Jose College, Manila; and died in Manila.

TEODORA ALONSO (1827-1913)

Mother of Jose Rizal who was the second child of Lorenzo Alonso and Brijida de Quintos. She studied at the Colegio de Santa Rosa. She was a business-minded woman. She was born in Santa Cruz, Manila on November 14, 1827 and died in 1913 in Manila.

SATURNINA RIZAL (1850-1913)

Eldest child of the Rizal-Alonzo marriage. Married Manuel Timoteo Hidalgo of Tanauan, Batangas.

PACIANO RIZAL (1851-1930)

Only brother of Jose Rizal and the second child. Studied at San Jose College in Manila; became a farmer and later a general of the Philippine Revolution.

NARCISA RIZAL (1852-1939)

The third child. married Antonio Lopez at Morong, Rizal; a teacher and musician.

OLYMPIA RIZAL (1855-1887)

The fourth child. Married Silvestre Ubaldo; died in 1887 from childbirth.

LUCIA RIZAL (1857-1919)

The fifth child. Married Matriano Herbosa.

MARIA RIZAL (1859-1945)

The sixth child. Married Daniel Faustino Cruz of Biñan, Laguna.

JOSE RIZAL (1861-1896)

The second son and the seventh child. He was executed by the Spaniards on December 30,1896.

CONCEPCION RIZAL (1862-1865)

The eight child. Died at the age of three.

JOSEFA RIZAL (1865-1945)

The ninth child. An epileptic, died a spinster.

TRINIDAD RIZAL (1868-1951)

The tenth child. Died a spinster and the last of the family to die.

SOLEDAD RIZAL (1870-1929)

The youngest child of the Rizal family. Being a teacher, she was arguably the best educated among Rizal’s sisters.

Education


First teacher

Jose Rizal’s first teacher was his mother, who had taught him how to read and pray and who had encouraged him to write poetry. Later, private tutors taught the young Rizal Spanish and Latin, before he was sent to a private school in Biñan.

Ateneo Municipal de Manila
When he was 11 years old, Rizal entered the Ateneo Municipal de Manila. He earned excellent marks in subjects like philosophy, physics, chemistry, and natural history. At this school, he read novels; wrote prize-winning poetry (and even a melodrama—“Junto al Pasig”); and practiced drawing, painting, and clay modeling, all of which remained lifelong interests for him.
Univerity of Sto. Tomas
Rizal eventually earned a land surveyor’s and assessor’s degree from the Ateneo Municipal while taking up Philosophy and Letters at the University of Santo Tomas. Upon learning that his mother was going blind, Rizal opted to study ophthalmology at the UST Faculty of Medicine and Surgery. He, however, was not able to complete the course because “he became politically isolated by adversaries among the faculty and clergy who demanded that he assimilate to their system.”
Universidad Central de Madrid
Without the knowledge of his parents, Rizal traveled to Europe in May 1882. According to his biographer, Austin Craig, Rizal, “in order to obtain a better education, had had to leave his country stealthily like a fugitive from justice, and his family, to save themselves from persecution, were compelled to profess ignorance of his plans and movements. His name was entered in Santo Tomas at the opening of the new term, with the fees paid, and Paciano had gone to Manila pretending to be looking for Rizal earned a Licentiate in Medicine at the Universidad Central de Madrid, where he also took courses in philosophy and literature. It was in Madrid that he conceived of writing Noli Me Tangere. He also attended the University of Paris and, in 1887, completed his eye specialization course at the University of Heidelberg. It was also in that year that Rizal’s first novel was published (in Berlin).

Rizal is said to have had the ability to master various skills, subjects, and languages. Our national hero was also a doctor, farmer, naturalist (he discovered the Draco rizali, a small lizard; Apogania rizali, a beetle; and the Rhacophorus rizali, a frog), writer, visual artist, athlete (martial arts, fencing, and pistol shooting), musician, and social scientist.

Works

Rizal's works include novels such as Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, which are considered some of the most important works in Filipino literature.

Noli Me Tangere (Touch Me Not)

Noli Me Tangere (Touch Me Not): Published in 1887, "Noli Me Tangere" is Rizal's first novel and is considered one of the most influential works of Philippine literature. It exposed the injustices and abuses of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines, particularly the oppressive social system and religious hypocrisy. The novel follows the story of Juan Crisostomo Ibarra, a young Filipino who returns to the Philippines from Europe, only to witness the corruption and oppression prevalent in his homeland.

El Filibusterismo (The Filibuster)

Published in 1891 as a sequel to "Noli Me Tangere," "El Filibusterismo" delves deeper into the themes of social injustice and oppression. The novel explores the consequences of colonial abuses and the failure of peaceful reform efforts, ultimately leading to a tragic and violent conclusion. Like its predecessor, "El Filibusterismo" is a scathing critique of Spanish colonial rule and its impact on Filipino society.

Mi Ultimo Adios (My Last Farewell)

Written on the eve of his execution, "Mi Ultimo Adios" is Rizal's farewell poem to his beloved homeland, the Philippines. It expresses his love for his country and his willingness to sacrifice his life for its freedom and independence. The poem has become one of the most iconic and revered works of Philippine literature, symbolizing Rizal's martyrdom and his enduring legacy as a national hero.

Essays and Articles

Rizal was also a prolific essayist and journalist, writing numerous articles and essays on a wide range of topics, including education, politics, history, and culture. His essays, such as "The Indolence of the Filipino," "The Philippines A Century Hence," and "To the Filipino Youth," continue to be studied and discussed for their insights into Philippine society and the challenges facing the Filipino people.

Poetry

Rizal was a talented poet who wrote poetry in both Spanish and Tagalog. His poems, such as "Sa Aking Mga Kabata" (To My Fellow Children) and "A La Juventud Filipina" (To the Filipino Youth), reflect his love for his country and his hopes for its future. His poetry celebrates the beauty of the Philippines and the resilience of its people in the face of adversity.

Travels Abroad

Jose Rizal traveled abroad primarily for education, activism, and exposure to Enlightenment ideas. His travels to Europe allowed him to pursue higher education, engage in socio-political activities advocating for reforms in the Philippines, and gain exposure to liberal and nationalist ideologies. Rizal utilized his time abroad for research, academic pursuits, and cultural exchange, which shaped his nationalist consciousness and commitment to social reform.

First Trip Abroad
1883-1887

May 1882: Rizal left the Philippines for Spain.
June 1882: Arrived in Madrid, Spain, to pursue higher education.
1882-1884: Studied at the Universidad Central de Madrid, focusing on medicine and liberal arts.
1885: Moved to Paris, France, to continue studies in ophthalmology at the University of Paris.
1886: Conducted research on the human eye and wrote essays on Philippine history and culture. 1887: Completed studies in ophthalmology and traveled to Germany for further studies.

Travel to Germany
1887-1888

1887:Rizal traveled to Germany to further his studies in ophthalmology at the University of Heidelberg.
During his time in Germany, he conducted research on the human eye and wrote several essays and articles on Philippine history and culture.

Travel across AEurope
1888-1891

1888-1889:After completing his studies in Germany, Rizal embarked on a tour of Europe, visiting various cities such as Berlin, Leipzig, Dresden, Prague, Vienna, and Brussels.

Return to the Philippines
1892

June 1892: Rizal returned to the Philippines amidst growing tensions between Filipino reformists and Spanish authorities.
Advocated for peaceful reforms but became increasingly critical of Spanish colonial rule.

Second Trip Abroad
1896

February 1896: Rizal was exiled to Dapitan, Mindanao, by Spanish authorities. Continued his scientific and humanitarian works while in exile.
December 1896: Arrested in Dapitan and transported to Manila to stand trial for his alleged involvement in the Philippine Revolution.

Homecoming and Execution
1896

December 1896: Rizal returned to Manila and was imprisoned at Fort Santiago.
December 30, 1896: Executed by firing squad at Bagumbayan (now Luneta Park) in Manila, sparking outrage and fueling the Philippine Revolution.

Oganizations

Jose Rizal was involved in several organizations during his lifetime. These organizations served various purposes and allowed Rizal to contribute to different causes. Some of the notable organizations associated with Jose Rizal include:

La Solidaridad

Rizal was a prominent member of La Solidaridad, a Filipino reformist organization founded in 1889 in Spain. The organization advocated for political reforms in the Philippines, including representation in the Spanish Cortes, equality before the law, and the abolition of the oppressive colonial policies.

Kidlat Club

Also known as the "Thunderbolt Club," it was a literary and social club founded by Rizal and his friends in Madrid, Spain, during his time as a student there.

La Liga Filipina

La Liga Filipina aimed to unite Filipinos and promote civic consciousness and social reforms peacefully. However, the organization was short-lived due to Rizal's arrest and subsequent exile

Propaganda Movement

An organization dedicated to promoting reforms in the Philippines and raising awareness about the country's situation in Spain and abroad. Rizal became involved in this committee during his time in Spain.

Masonic Orders

Rizal was a Freemason and belonged to various Masonic lodges, including Logia Solidaridad No. 53 in Madrid and Nilad Lodge No. 144 in the Philippines. Freemasonry played a significant role in the intellectual and political awakening of many Filipino nationalists during Rizal's time.

Association Hispano-Filipino

This Filipino student organization in Madrid aimed to foster camaraderie among Filipino students studying in Spain and discuss issues related to the Philippines and Spanish colonial rule.
